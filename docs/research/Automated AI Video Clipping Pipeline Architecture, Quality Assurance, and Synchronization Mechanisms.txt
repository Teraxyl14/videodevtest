2. The Physics of Digital Video and the "Freeze Frame" Anomaly
To resolve the "Freeze Frame at Start" issue, one must first deconstruct the anatomy of a compressed video stream and how FFmpeg interacts with it during seek operations. The phenomenon described—where a cut video freezes on a single frame while audio plays, or starts with black—is a deterministic result of mishandling Group of Pictures (GOP) structures, Presentation Time Stamps (PTS), and the specific limitations of the MP4 container format.
2.1. Inter-Frame Compression and the GOP Structure
Modern video codecs utilize temporal compression to achieve high efficiency. Instead of storing every frame as a complete image (Intra-coding), they store a sequence of dependencies. This sequence is known as the Group of Pictures (GOP). Understanding the GOP is critical to explaining why simple stream copying fails at arbitrary cut points.
A typical GOP structure consists of three frame types:
* I-Frames (Intra-coded): These are self-contained images that require no other data to decode. They are essentially JPEGs embedded in the video stream. They serve as the anchor points for the video, often referred to as Keyframes or IDR (Instantaneous Decoder Refresh) frames.
* P-Frames (Predicted): These frames hold only the difference (residuals) from the previous I-frame or P-frame. They cannot be displayed unless the preceding frames have been successfully decoded.
* B-Frames (Bi-directional Predicted): These frames hold differences from both past and future frames, offering the highest compression but introducing decoding latency and complexity.
The "Open GOP" Problem and Cut Boundaries
The user's issue primarily stems from attempting to cut a video at a timestamp $T_{cut}$ that falls inside a GOP, rather than at an I-Frame boundary.
When a command like ffmpeg -ss 15 -i input.mp4 -c copy is executed, the user requests a cut at 15 seconds. However, if the GOP size is 250 frames (standard for many web videos, equating to 10 seconds at 25fps ), there might be an I-frame at 10 seconds and another at 20 seconds. The frame at 15 seconds is likely a P-frame or B-frame.
1. Stream Copy Limitation: In -c copy mode, FFmpeg functions as a packet mover, not a pixel generator. It simply copies the compressed bitstream packets from input to output. It cannot mathematically reconstruct the pixel data at 15 seconds because it is forbidden from running the decoder. Consequently, it cannot generate a new I-frame at the 15-second mark.
2. The "Snap-to-Keyframe" Behavior: To produce a valid bitstream that a player can theoretically decode, FFmpeg defaults to including the nearest I-frame before the seek point. In our example, it includes the I-frame at 10 seconds and all subsequent P/B frames up to 15 seconds.
3. The Playback Freeze: The output file physically contains video data starting from timestamp 10s. However, the container metadata (specifically the Edit List atom in MP4) instructs the player to skip presentation until timestamp 15s.
   * Scenario A: The player honors the edit list but the decoder is slow. It decodes 10s-15s in the background, showing a frozen first frame (or black) until the clock hits 15s.
   * Scenario B: The player ignores the edit list and plays from 10s, resulting in the clip starting earlier than intended.
   * Scenario C (The "Negative Timestamp" Issue): If FFmpeg attempts to offset the timestamps to make 15s the new "0s", the frames from 10s to 15s effectively have negative timestamps (e.g., -5s). Many players clamp negative timestamps to zero, causing the video to freeze on the first available frame (the I-frame at -5s/10s) until the audio catches up to the first positive timestamp.
2.2. Timestamp Mechanics: PTS vs. DTS
Deepening the analysis requires distinguishing between Decoding Time Stamps (DTS) and Presentation Time Stamps (PTS).
* DTS: Tells the decoder when to decode a frame. Because B-frames require future frames to be decoded first, the decoding order differs from the viewing order.
* PTS: Tells the player when to display the frame on screen.
When cutting with -c copy, the relationship between DTS and PTS is preserved relative to the original stream. If the cut segment starts at P-frame 15s (which depends on I-frame 10s), the I-frame must be decoded at $DTS=10$ but the P-frame is presented at $PTS=15$. If the new file forces the start time to 0, the PTS/DTS values must be rewritten. If this rewriting is mishandled (common in simple FFmpeg commands), the delta between DTS and PTS can result in decoder buffer underflows or persistent freeze frames as the playback engine waits for the "correct" time to arrive.
2.3. The Solution: Mandatory Re-encoding for Frame Accuracy
Given the user's explicit objective of "Robust Accurate Seeking & Cutting," referencing and , relying on stream copy (-c copy) is structurally untenable for an automated pipeline that requires arbitrary cut points dictated by AI models. Automation implies cuts determined by content (e.g., "start when the person speaks"), which rarely aligns with the rigid 10-second intervals of IDR frames.
Therefore, the pipeline must abandon stream copy for the cut operation and implement a transcoding step. While this incurs a computational cost, it is the only way to guarantee that the first frame of the new clip is a valid I-frame, eliminating the freeze.
Hardware Acceleration as a Mitigating Factor
To offset the performance penalty of re-encoding, the pipeline should leverage hardware acceleration where available (NVIDIA NVENC, Intel QSV). However, the focus of this report is on the correctness of the signal flow, which remains constant regardless of the encoder implementation. The command structure proposed in Section 6 will focus on libx264 for maximum compatibility, but the logic applies equally to hardware encoders.

3. Advanced FFmpeg Seeking & Cutting Mechanics
The implementation of the cut requires a nuanced understanding of FFmpeg's seeking algorithms. The user query highlights the need to compare -ss positions and audio re-encoding.
3.1. Input Seeking vs. Output Seeking
The placement of the -ss argument fundamentally alters how FFmpeg processes the input file. This distinction is crucial for optimizing the trade-off between speed and accuracy.
Input Seeking (-ss before -i)
* Command: ffmpeg -ss 00:10:00 -i input.mp4...
* Mechanism: FFmpeg uses the demuxer to parse the file's index (atom table) and jumps directly to the nearest keyframe before the target timestamp.
* Performance: Extremely fast ($O(1)$ relative to file size), as it skips decoding the preceding gigabytes of data.
* Accuracy Profile:
   * With -c copy: Inaccurate. It snaps to the nearest keyframe (e.g., 00:09:55).
   * With Re-encoding: Frame-Accurate. Since FFmpeg 2.1, when re-encoding is active, input seeking acts as a "fast seek to keyframe" followed by a "decode and discard" operation. FFmpeg seeks to 00:09:55, decodes the frames until it hits 00:10:00, and only then begins the output pipeline. This provides the speed of seeking with the accuracy of decoding.
Output Seeking (-ss after -i)
* Command: ffmpeg -i input.mp4 -ss 00:10:00...
* Mechanism: FFmpeg starts decoding from the very first frame of the video ($T=0$) and processes every single frame up to the target timestamp, discarding them along the way.
* Performance: Extremely slow ($O(N)$), proportional to the seek position. Seeking to hour 2 of a video requires decoding 2 hours of footage.
* Accuracy Profile: Frame-perfect in all scenarios, as the decoder state is fully consistent.
Combined Seeking (The Hybrid Approach)
For extremely long files where the "decode and discard" phase of input seeking might still take a second or two (if the GOP is massive), a hybrid approach is sometimes used: ffmpeg -ss [target-30s] -i input.mp4 -ss 30... However, for modern libx264 outputs with standard GOP sizes (2-10 seconds), Input Seeking with Re-encoding is the industry standard for automated pipelines. It is fast, accurate, and robust.
3.2. Audio Sync and the "Priming" Artifacts
A subtle but critical source of desync in automated clipping arises from audio codec latency, specifically in AAC (Advanced Audio Coding).
The Priming Problem
AAC is a transform-based codec (MDCT). It requires a sequence of samples to "warm up" or "prime" the filter bank before it can output valid audio. This is typically 1024 or 2112 samples (approx. 20-45ms at 48kHz). When a video is cut using FFmpeg, the video stream might be cut precisely at frame boundaries. However, if the audio is stream-copied or processed without accounting for this priming delay, the new audio stream may start with silent "padding" samples that push the actual audio 40ms late relative to the video. Over a series of concatenated clips, this drift can become noticeable.
The Fix: asetpts and Re-encoding
To guarantee sync, the audio must be re-encoded (-c:a aac) alongside the video. Furthermore, explicitly resetting the audio timestamps is required to flush any container offsets.
* Video Filter: setpts=PTS-STARTPTS (Resets video clock to 0).
* Audio Filter: asetpts=PTS-STARTPTS (Resets audio clock to 0). Using these filters ensures that both streams mathematically start at $T=0.000$ in the new container, discarding any pre-roll or priming offsets.
3.3. Timestamp Manifold: avoid_negative_ts
When utilizing input seeking, FFmpeg essentially "shifts" the time base. If the internal logic calculates the cut start time as prior to the first decoded frame (a common rounding error in floating-point timestamp math), the resulting timestamps can be negative.
* make_zero: This argument is mandatory for the pipeline. It shifts timestamps so that the first timestamp is exactly 0. This prevents the "black screen at start" issue where players wait for positive time.
* auto: The default behavior, which is often insufficient for rigid automated pipelines that feed into downstream ML models requiring precise $T=0$ alignment.

4. Temporal Alignment: Captions & PyCaps
The second objective is Timestamp Alignment for Captions. The challenge is mathematically translating the temporal metadata of a subtitle file (SRT/VTT/JSON) from the source timeline to the clip timeline.
4.1. The Desynchronization Vector
If a transcript generated by PyCaps (wrapping Whisper) identifies a word "Hello" at 00:05:00.000, and the automated pipeline cuts the video from 00:04:55.000 to 00:05:05.000, the caption file is now invalid. The new video has a duration of 10 seconds. The caption "Hello" must ideally appear at 00:00:05.000.
The Fundamental Offset Formula:
$$T_{new} = T_{original} - T_{cut\_start}$$
However, simply subtracting the offset is insufficient due to three complexities:
1. Boundary Straddling: A caption might start before the cut ($T < 4:55$) and end inside it ($T > 4:55$).
2. VAD Offset: Voice Activity Detection often trims silence from the audio chunk before processing, introducing a hidden latency.
3. Drift: Clock skew between the subtitle timebase and the video timebase.
4.2. PyCaps Implementation Logic
PyCaps generates objects with absolute start and end times. The pipeline must function as a temporal filter that ingests the full transcript and emits a retimed fragment.
Algorithm for Transcript Alignment
1. Ingest: Load the full transcript object $T_{full}$.
2. Define Window: Establish the valid time domain $W =$.
3. Iterative Filtering: Loop through all caption segments $S_i$.
   * Exclusion:
      * If $S_i.end < T_{cut\_start}$: Discard (Pre-clip).
      * If $S_i.start > T_{cut\_end}$: Discard (Post-clip).
   * Intersection Handling (The Straddle Case):
      * If $S_i.start < T_{cut\_start}$ but $S_i.end > T_{cut\_start}$: The word began before the cut.
      * Policy Decision: For automated clips, it is usually cleaner to clamp the start time to 0 rather than discard the word, ensuring the viewer sees the text for the audio that is audible.
      * $S_i.start_{new} = \max(0, S_i.start_{old} - T_{cut\_start})$.
   * Standard Shifting:
      * $S_i.end_{new} = S_i.end_{old} - T_{cut\_start}$.
      * $S_i.end_{new} = \min(T_{duration}, S_i.end_{new})$ (Clamp end to clip duration).
4. Validation: Ensure $S_i.end_{new} > S_i.start_{new}$ (reject zero-length captions).
4.3. The "Processing Delay" Desync
Research snippets and indicate that subtitle desync is often caused by a mismatch in how silence is handled. If PyCaps/Whisper runs on an audio chunk that was pre-processed (e.g., silence removal), the timestamps it returns are relative to the shortened audio. Critical Insight: The pipeline must ensure that PyCaps is run on the exact audio stream that FFmpeg is cutting, or the offsets must account for any VAD trimming. The safest architectural approach is to run PyCaps on the original source file to get absolute timestamps, and then apply the mathematical offset logic described above. Running PyCaps on the cut clip often fails because the short duration lacks enough context for the Transformer model to establish accurate timing (hallucination risk).

5. Computer Vision & Object Tracking (YOLOv11 to 9:16 Crop)
The third objective is Robust Crop Window Calculation. The goal is to convert a landscape (16:9) video into a portrait (9:16) video by dynamically cropping around a subject detected by YOLOv11. This is a "Virtual Cameraman" problem.
5.1. The Coordinate Transformation
Input Parameters:
* Source Dimensions: $W_{src} = 1920$, $H_{src} = 1080$.
* YOLO Bounding Box: $(x_1, y_1, x_2, y_2)$.
* Subject Center ($C_x, C_y$): $C_x = \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}$.
Target Parameters:
* Target Aspect Ratio: $9:16$.
* Crop Height ($H_{crop}$): Typically equal to $H_{src}$ (1080) to maximize resolution, though this can be scaled down if digital zoom is desired.
* Crop Width ($W_{crop}$): $H_{crop} \times \frac{9}{16} = 1080 \times 0.5625 \approx 608$ pixels.
The Naive Calculation Failure:
A direct mapping of the YOLO center to the crop center follows this logic:
$$X_{crop} = C_x - \frac{W_{crop}}{2}$$
$$Y_{crop} = 0$$
Implementing this raw calculation leads to Tracking Failure/Centering Issues for three reasons:
1. High-Frequency Jitter: YOLO detections are probabilistic. Even a stationary subject will have a bounding box that fluctuates by 5-10 pixels per frame. Mapping this directly to the crop window causes the entire video frame to shake, inducing motion sickness.
2. Boundary Violations: If the subject moves to the far left of the source frame, $X_{crop}$ becomes negative. If they move far right, $X_{crop} + W_{crop} > W_{src}$. This results in FFmpeg errors or black bars.
3. Tracking Loss: If YOLO fails to detect the subject for a single frame (occlusion or motion blur), $C_x$ becomes undefined or jumps to a default value, causing a massive, jarring camera cut.
5.2. Signal Smoothing: Control Theory for Virtual Cameras
To create a professional "Virtual Cameraman" feel, the pipeline must implement signal smoothing. The position of the crop window should behave like a physical camera on a fluid head tripod—reacting to intentional movement but ignoring micro-jitters.
Why Simple Moving Average (SMA) Fails
A common mistake is using a Simple Moving Average (mean of the last $N$ frames). SMA introduces linear latency. To sufficiently smooth the jitter, one might need a window of 30 frames (1 second). However, if the subject suddenly dashes to the right, the "camera" will wait 0.5-1.0 seconds before starting to move, causing the subject to exit the frame. This is unacceptable for active subjects.
The Kalman Filter
The Kalman Filter is a recursive estimator that predicts the state of a system (position and velocity) and corrects it with noisy measurements. It is excellent for modeling linear motion.
* Mechanism: It maintains a covariance matrix estimating the uncertainty of the position. When the measurement variance is high (noisy YOLO), it relies more on its internal prediction (physics model).
* Limitations: Tuning the process noise ($Q$) and measurement noise ($R$) matrices is non-trivial and often scene-dependent. It can also "overshoot" when a subject stops abruptly.
The One Euro Filter (Recommended Solution)
The One Euro Filter is an adaptive low-pass filter specifically tailored for human-computer interaction and noisy input signals where low latency is critical. It is the industry standard for cursor tracking and VR head tracking, making it ideal for this pipeline.
* Adaptive Cutoff: The core innovation is that the filter's cutoff frequency ($f_c$) is not constant. It varies based on the velocity of the signal.
   * Low Velocity (Stationary Subject): $f_c$ decreases. The filter becomes very aggressive, eliminating almost all jitter. The shot looks locked-off.
   * High Velocity (Moving Subject): $f_c$ increases. The filter reduces smoothing to minimize latency. The camera snaps to the subject's new position almost instantly.
* Equation:
$$f_c = f_{min} + \beta \cdot |\dot{x}|$$
Where $f_{min}$ is the minimum cutoff (e.g., 1.0 Hz) and $\beta$ is the speed coefficient.
* Superiority: Unlike the Kalman filter, the One Euro Filter is easy to tune (only two parameters: min_cutoff and beta) and handles the "start-stop" nature of human motion more naturally than a constant-velocity Kalman model.
5.3. Handling Tracking Failures (Occlusion)
When YOLO returns no detections for frame $N$:
   1. Hold Last State: The One Euro Filter should process the last known position but with a highly reduced cutoff frequency, effectively "freezing" the camera pan until the subject reappears.
   2. Ghost Detection: If the subject disappears for $> T_{threshold}$ (e.g., 2 seconds), the logic should smoothly drift the crop back to the center of the frame or switch to a "wide" crop if resolution allows.

        Returns a subset of the transcript aligned to the new 0-based timeline.

            seg_start = segment['start']
            seg_end = segment['end']

            if seg_end < start_time:
                continue # Happened before clip
            if seg_start > end_time:
                break    # Happened after clip (assuming sorted input)
                
            # 2. Calculate new relative timestamps
            # Math: T_new = T_old - Cut_Start
            new_start = seg_start - start_time
            new_end = seg_end - start_time
            
            # 3. Handle Boundary Straddling (Clamping)
            # If a word starts before 0, clamp it to 0 so it appears immediately.
            new_start = max(0.0, new_start)
            # If a word ends after the clip finishes, clamp it to duration.
            new_end = min(duration, new_end)
            
            # 4. Validation
            # Filter out micro-segments that might result from clamping (e.g., 0.01s duration)
            if new_end - new_start > 0.1: 
                aligned.append({
                    'text': segment['text'],
                    'start': round(new_start, 3), # Rounding for clean VTT/SRT output
                    'end': round(new_end, 3)
                })
                
        return aligned


6.3. Reframing Math with One Euro Filter Implementation
This section provides the exact Python implementation of the One Euro Filter and the bounds-checking logic for the crop window. This satisfies the requirement for "Coordinate Space & Tracking Logic".

import math
import time


class OneEuroFilter:
    def __init__(self, t0, x0, min_cutoff=1.0, beta=0.0, d_cutoff=1.0):
        """
        Initialize the One Euro Filter.
        min_cutoff: Min cutoff frequency in Hz. Lower = more smoothing (less jitter).
        beta: Speed coefficient. Higher = less lag (faster response).
        d_cutoff: Cutoff for the derivative (usually 1.0 Hz).
        """
        self.min_cutoff = float(min_cutoff)
        self.beta = float(beta)
        self.d_cutoff = float(d_cutoff)
        
        # State variables
        self.x_prev = float(x0)
        self.dx_prev = 0.0
        self.t_prev = float(t0)


    def smoothing_factor(self, t_e, cutoff):
        r = 2 * math.pi * cutoff * t_e
        return r / (r + 1)


    def exponential_smoothing(self, a, x, x_prev):
        return a * x + (1 - a) * x_prev


    def __call__(self, t, x):
        """
        Filter a new value x at time t.
        """
        t_e = t - self.t_prev
        
        # Handle duplicate timestamps or backward time jumps
        if t_e <= 0:
            return self.x_prev


        # 1. Estimate the derivative (velocity) of the signal
        a_d = self.smoothing_factor(t_e, self.d_cutoff)
        dx = (x - self.x_prev) / t_e
        dx_hat = self.exponential_smoothing(a_d, dx, self.dx_prev)


        # 2. Calculate the adaptive cutoff frequency
        # The faster the signal moves, the higher the cutoff (less smoothing)
        cutoff = self.min_cutoff + self.beta * abs(dx_hat)
        
        # 3. Filter the signal
        a = self.smoothing_factor(t_e, cutoff)
        x_hat = self.exponential_smoothing(a, x, self.x_prev)


        # Update state
        self.x_prev = x_hat
        self.dx_prev = dx_hat
        self.t_prev = t
        
        return x_hat


def calculate_crop_window(frame_idx, detections, video_w, video_h, smoother):
    """
    Calculates the 9:16 crop window based on YOLO detections.
    """
    # Target Dimensions (9:16)
    # We maintain full height (1080) and crop width.
    target_h = video_h
    target_w = int(video_h * (9/16)) # e.g., 608 pixels for 1080p
    
    # 1. Get Subject Center (x)
    # detections format: list of [x1, y1, x2, y2, confidence, class_id]
    if detections:
        # Strategy: Pick largest box (assume main subject is largest)
        # Alternative: Pick box closest to center, or specific class ID
        box = max(detections, key=lambda b: (b-b) * (b-b))
        center_x = (box + box) / 2
    else:
        # Fallback: If tracking lost, drift towards center
        # Ideally, this should be the 'last known position' passed in via state
        center_x = video_w / 2


    # 2. Apply One Euro Filter
    # We use frame_idx as time. If fps is 30, t = frame_idx / 30.0
    # Here we simplify and just use frame_idx if the filter is tuned for it.
    smoothed_x = smoother(frame_idx, center_x)


    # 3. Calculate Top-Left Corner
    crop_x = smoothed_x - (target_w / 2)
    crop_y = 0 # Vertical center usually not needed if using full height
    
    # 4. Rigid Bounds Checking (Clamping)
    # Ensure the crop window never leaves the video canvas
    if crop_x < 0:
        crop_x = 0
    elif crop_x + target_w > video_w:
        crop_x = video_w - target_w
        
    return int(crop_x), int(crop_y), target_w, target_h

7. Strategic Implications and Further Optimizations
7.1. Variable Frame Rate (VFR) Handling
A common edge case in web-scraped video is Variable Frame Rate (VFR). If the source video drops frames to save bandwidth during static scenes, the frame_idx logic in the tracking smoother will desynchronize from real-time.
   * Implication: The One Euro Filter relies on $t_e$ (time elapsed). If frames are processed sequentially but represent varying time deltas, the velocity calculation $|\dot{x}|$ will be incorrect.
   * Correction: In the OneEuroFilter.__call__, pass the actual timestamp of the frame (from cv2.CAP_PROP_POS_MSEC) rather than the frame index.
7.2. "Lookahead" Smoothing (Non-Causal Filtering)
The One Euro Filter is a "causal" filter—it only knows the past. It must react to movement, implying intrinsic lag. However, since this is an automated post-processing pipeline (not a live stream), we have access to the future.
   * Optimization: Run YOLO on the entire clip first to generate a full trajectory array $X_{raw}$.
   * Technique: Apply a Savitzky-Golay filter or a Forward-Backward Kalman Smoother (RTS Smoother).
   * Benefit: This allows the virtual camera to start moving before the subject moves, simulating a professional camera operator who anticipates the action. This eliminates the "lag" inherent in causal filters entirely.
7.3. Hardware Acceleration Integration
For high-volume pipelines, CPU-based libx264 encoding is a bottleneck. The smart_cut_video function can be adapted for NVIDIA GPUs using h264_nvenc:

   2. Logic: [0:v]subtitles=subs.srt[v_sub]; [v_sub]crop=w:h:x:y[out]

   3. Correct Logic: The subtitle renderer needs to know the crop boundaries, or the subtitles must be rendered after cropping. However, libass (FFmpeg's subtitle renderer) usually expects the input resolution to match the subtitle file's coordinate system.
   * Recommendation: Generate the subtitle file, then use a complex filtergraph to crop the video first, then scale/pad if necessary, and finally burn subtitles, or ensure the subtitle styles (margins) force the text into the center 9:16 area before burning.
8. Conclusion
The "Freeze Frame" anomaly in the user's pipeline is a structural byproduct of stream-copying modern video codecs at non-IDR points; the definitive solution is robust re-encoding with explicit timestamp resets via PTS-STARTPTS and avoid_negative_ts. Subtitle desynchronization is a linear offset problem that requires strictly subtracting the cut start time from absolute timestamps derived from the source file, not the cut file. Finally, the erratic tracking of raw YOLO detections is resolved not by improving the detection model, but by applying control theory—specifically the One Euro Filter—to the coordinate trajectory.
By replacing the simplistic subprocess calls with the smart_cut_video logic and wrapping the YOLO detections in the OneEuroFilter, the pipeline will transition from a fragile prototype to a production-grade automated editing system capable of producing broadcast-quality vertical video.
